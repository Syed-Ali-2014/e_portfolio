Case Study: Inappropriate Use of Surveys

In 2018, **Cambridge Analytica** was involved in one of the largest data misuse scandals in recent history (Confessore, 2018). The company used seemingly innocuous personality quizzes and surveys on Facebook to harvest personal data from millions of users, which was then weaponized for targeted political advertising, particularly during the 2016 U.S. presidential election and the Brexit referendum. This case highlighted the dangers of how survey data can be misused when ethical boundaries are not maintained.

Cambridge Analytica: Ethical, Legal, and Social Implications

  • Ethical Concerns: Users were unaware that their data would be used beyond the immediate scope of the personality quizzes they participated in. This raised significant concerns about **informed consent** and the lack of transparency in data usage. Participants did not know their responses would be used for political manipulation and commercial purposes.
  • Legal Issues: The scandal led to multiple legal actions, including a $5 billion fine for Facebook from the U.S. Federal Trade Commission (FTC) for violating user privacy. It also intensified scrutiny around global data protection regulations, particularly the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) in the EU.
  • Social Impact: Public trust in social media platforms was deeply eroded, with many users questioning how their data was being collected, shared, and monetized. The scandal further fueled debates about data privacy and surveillance capitalism.
  • Professional Responsibility: Data scientists, survey designers, and platform engineers faced severe criticism for their roles in enabling such practices. The case emphasized the need for data professionals to adhere to stricter ethical guidelines when handling sensitive user data.

Further Examples of Inappropriate Survey Use

Example 1: Nielsen Ratings and Television Viewer Tracking

Nielsen, a company known for tracking television viewership, has also been criticized for using surveys to gather data that extends beyond television preferences. In 2015, Nielsen was accused of collecting personal data without properly disclosing the extent to which the information was used for advertising purposes. Although the survey was designed to track viewing habits, the data was later sold to third-party marketers without explicit user consent, raising concerns about transparency and the commodification of personal information.

Example 2: Phishing Surveys for Data Breaches

In the healthcare sector, fake patient satisfaction surveys have been used as a tactic to gather sensitive information. For instance, in 2020, a phishing scam posed as a legitimate health organization, asking patients to fill out a survey. The survey requested personal information like social security numbers and medical insurance details, which were later used for identity theft. This demonstrates how seemingly legitimate surveys can be weaponized to deceive users into disclosing highly sensitive personal information.

Key Lessons and Recommendations

  • Transparency in Data Collection: Surveys must provide clear information about how the data will be used, who will have access to it, and how long it will be retained. Participants must be informed of their rights to opt out and have their data deleted if necessary.
  • Ethical Guidelines: Survey creators and data analysts should follow stringent ethical standards, ensuring that users' data is protected and used solely for the stated purpose of the survey. Professional organizations should enforce these standards through certification and compliance checks.
  • Legal Safeguards: Data protection regulations like GDPR and the California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA) must be strictly adhered to in all survey designs. Surveys that collect data from vulnerable populations (e.g., healthcare, children, etc.) should be subject to even higher standards of scrutiny.
  • Professional Responsibility: Data professionals, particularly those handling survey responses, must be trained in ethical data management, ensuring that they handle data with care and transparency, avoiding exploitation or misuse.
